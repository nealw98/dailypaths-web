Step Three

FAITH & THE FREEDOM OF LETTING GO

Made a decision to turn our will and our lives over to the care of God as we understood Him.

By the time we reach Step Three, many of us have discovered a gentler rhythm in recovery. Step One showed us our limits—the painful truth that we can’t control alcohol or those affected by it. Step Two brought a dawning hope that help exists beyond ourselves. But even with that hope, our instinct to hold on can be fierce. We still find ourselves managing, planning, and worrying, as if constant effort could protect what we love. Step Three is where we learn to let go, not as a theory but as a daily way of living.

This step invites a simple but profound shift: to stop trying to do what we now understand we cannot. We remember what we’ve learned in Al‑Anon—the 3 C’s. We didn’t cause the drinking, we can’t control it, and we can’t cure it. Carrying those truths into Step Three, we begin to see that continued control isn’t love—it’s fear. Letting go becomes the only act of trust that makes sense.

Made a Decision

Step Three begins with a decision, though not the kind we make with bold confidence. This decision often starts quietly, with a sigh of surrender after trying everything else. It’s the willingness to hand over whatever we can’t carry—our need to fix, to manage, to direct life according to our own script. It’s not a one‑time promise; it’s a choice we make again and again, sometimes many times a day.

Early on, our decisions might be small. We may decide not to remind someone of a consequence they already know. We may stop checking if a drinker is telling the truth. We may pause before reacting, allowing space for calm to guide us instead of fear. Each small act of release becomes practice for the larger letting‑go that Step Three teaches us.

For many, there’s an uneasy tug here. Letting go feels risky—if we don’t hold on, won’t everything fall apart? Recovery gently shows us that everything kept falling apart when we did hold on. The decision to turn our will over isn’t an act of recklessness but of sanity. It opens the door to guidance and stability far wiser than our own anxious striving.

Turning Our Will and Our Lives Over

Step Three asks us to turn both our will and our lives over. Our will is our thinking—the part of us that insists on control, solutions, and certainty. Our life is what follows that thinking—the emotions, the relationships, the exhaustion born of trying to be in charge of what isn’t ours.

When we begin to turn these over, we find relief. We learn that we can stop pushing outcomes and start responding to reality instead. It doesn’t mean we stop caring; it means we accept that caring and controlling are not the same thing.

Letting go can feel uncertain at first, like setting down a heavy load and discovering our hands don’t know what to do. But over time, we begin to feel the weight lift. The world doesn’t collapse. The people we worried about still find their way. And we start discovering something surprising—we have energy again. We have peace.

To the Care of God as We Understood Him

The heart of Step Three isn’t just “God” or “Power.” It’s care. The promise is that we can rest our will and our lives in something capable of caring for us better than we could on our own. For some of us, that sense of care begins in Al‑Anon meetings themselves—in the calm understanding we feel sitting among others who’ve been where we are. For others, it’s a quiet, personal sense of presence that grows with time.

The beauty of Step Three is that no one defines “God” for us. We each come to our own understanding, and it changes as we grow. What matters isn’t the name—but the experience. When we stop running the show and allow that caring presence to lead, we start to feel safe again. We start to trust life.

Turning things over doesn’t mean handing them to someone else to fix. It means trusting that we no longer have to manage the unmanageable—and that we are being cared for in ways we may not yet see.

Willingness, Trust, and Release

The spiritual principles of Step Three are simple but life‑changing: willingness, trust, and release. Willingness opens the door; trust helps us step through; release keeps us walking even when we can’t yet see the end of the path.

Each time we choose not to control, we practice Step Three. Each time we pause and breathe instead of reacting, we allow care to replace chaos. Over time, we notice that life flows more smoothly when we stop forcing our will. What once felt like loss begins to feel like freedom.

A Daily Decision

Step Three is rarely mastered—it’s lived, not completed. There will be days we take our will back, trying once again to manage outcomes or people. But now we notice the strain more quickly, and we know the way back. We remember the peace that comes from release.

When we make the decision to turn our will and our lives over to care, we begin to live in cooperation with something kinder than fear, steadier than control. Step Three is where recovery starts to breathe on its own, carrying us gently forward—one decision, one day, one letting‑go at a time.

Questions for Reflection

Take your time with these questions. There are no right answers — only honest ones.

  • What does “letting go” mean to me in practical terms?
  • In what areas of my life am I still trying to be in charge of outcomes?
  • What am I afraid will happen if I actually let go of this situation?
  • What does "Thy will, not mine" look like in my primary relationship?
