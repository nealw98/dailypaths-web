Accepting Al-Anon as It Is

Acceptance Tradition 3

Accepting Al-Anon as it is helps me to accept myself where I am.

Paths to Recovery, p. 160

I wanted Al-Anon to be different. Better organized. More focused. Less crying, more solutions. I had opinions about how meetings should run, what people should share, and how long they should take doing it. I walked in wanting help and immediately started redesigning the room.

Sound familiar? It should. It's exactly what I do everywhere else. Try to fix, improve, control, perfect. I couldn't even accept a recovery program without wanting to manage it first.

That's when the parallel hit me. Accepting Al-Anon as it is — messy, imperfect, sometimes frustrating — was practice for accepting myself the same way. If I couldn't sit in a meeting without mentally rewriting the format, how was I going to sit with my own flaws without trying to overhaul myself into someone unrecognizable?

Al-Anon doesn't need to be perfect to help me. The meeting that runs long still gives me something I need. The share that frustrates me sometimes holds the truth I'm resisting. The program in all its imperfection works — not despite the mess, but through it.

Same with me. I work. Not perfectly, not the way I'd design myself if I could, but well enough. Accepting that is harder than accepting any meeting format.

The next time I want to criticize a meeting, a share, or the program itself, I can use it as a mirror — where am I refusing to accept myself the same way? Then I can let both be imperfect and still useful.
